## 1. From the DGP to the population model

Essay 4 defined the primitive object of the program: a finite directed acyclic graph $G = (V, E)$ with local random observables $\{X_v\}$, an overlapping spacetime filtration $\{\mathcal{G}(v)\}$, and a system of axioms encoding causal structure and dependence. The key elements were: local and past $\sigma$-fields ($\mathcal{F}(v)$ and $\mathcal{G}(v)$), the partially overlapping structure of common and private pasts, conditional spacelike factorization (Axiom L1 — the DAG Markov property), cone-supported weak dependence (Axiom L2), and regularity conditions (G1–G3). The DAG structure already implied substantial consequences: Markov equivalence as a discrete gauge symmetry, faithfulness as the Hauptvermutung, and the Markov blanket as a causal horizon.

That was the DGP — the finite stochastic process that, in this program, constitutes the actual microscopic spacetime.

This essay takes the next step. It asks: how does the smooth Lorentzian manifold of general relativity emerge from the finite graph? In the econometric analogy that has organized this series, the finite graph is the sample, the continuum manifold is the population, and the passage between them is the population limit $n \to \infty$. Essay 1 established the analogy: QM is the finite-sample DGP (the sieve approximation with irreducible noise), GR is the population model (the smooth regression function). Essay 4 built the DGP explicitly. Now we recover the population model.

The reconstruction chain, from Essay 4, is:

$$\text{finite DAG + local } \sigma\text{-fields} \;\xrightarrow{\;n \to \infty\;}\; \text{continuum net} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{dependence cones} \;\Longrightarrow\; [g] \;\Longrightarrow\; g$$

This essay develops the three stages after the first arrow: convergence to a continuum net, recovery of conformal geometry from the support of dependence, and recovery of metric scale from the rate of dependence decay. It then examines the Cauchy-temporal filtration and the arrow of time, ventures — speculatively — toward dynamics, and confronts the boundary where classical dependence theory meets the quantum amplitude structure.

## 2. The population limit: from finite graphs to continuum nets

The finite causal-dependence network is the DGP. The smooth Lorentzian manifold is the population model. The passage between them is the population limit — and it is the most important step in the program.

Consider a sequence of finite DAGs $G_n = (V_n, E_n)$ with $|V_n| \to \infty$, each equipped with local observables $\{X_v^{(n)}\}$ and satisfying Axioms F1–G3. As $n$ grows, the graph becomes finer and denser — more spacetime atoms packed into the same "region." The graph distance $\delta$ between neighboring nodes shrinks; the rooted past subgraphs become deeper and more regular.

In the limit, the discrete structure should converge to a continuum object. The convergence is in the local convergence topology on rooted graphs (Benjamini and Schramm, 2001): for each "point" $p$ in the limit space, the rooted past subgraphs $G_{v_n}$ (for nodes $v_n$ converging to $p$) converge to a limit rooted graph $G_p$. If the axioms are satisfied uniformly along the sequence, the limit inherits a continuum structure.

Specifically, in the limit:

- The finite node set $V_n$ converges to a connected topological space $M$ — a manifold.
- The discrete $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{G}(v)$ converge to a net of local $\sigma$-fields $O \mapsto \mathcal{F}(O)$ for open regions $O \subset M$. This is the probabilistic analog of a Haag–Kastler net in algebraic quantum field theory (AQFT): each region $O$ is assigned a sub-$\sigma$-field $\mathcal{F}(O) \subseteq \mathscr{F}$, representing the information generated by all observables localized in $O$.
- The conditional $\alpha_C$ on the finite graph converges to a continuum $\alpha(O_1, O_2)$ defined on pairs of regions. In the continuum, the conditional factorization axiom (L1) takes a cleaner form: for spacelike-separated regions $O_1, O_2$ in the continuum net, the *local* $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{F}(O_1)$ and $\mathcal{F}(O_2)$ are independent. The conditioning on the common past, which was explicit in the discrete setting, is absorbed into the continuum net structure — the local $\sigma$-fields of spacelike-separated regions are defined to contain only the information generated *within* those regions, with the common past already factored out. This is exactly the locality axiom of the Haag–Kastler framework.
- The discrete $D_{\text{BL}}$ converges to a continuum $D_{\text{BL}}(O_1, O_2)$.
- The remaining axioms carry over: decaying dependence for causally related regions (L2), and regular cone structure (G1–G3).

The sieve analogy from Essay 1 is exact here. The finite graph is the sieve: a finite-dimensional approximation to the continuum, with $|V_n|$ playing the role of the number of basis functions. The continuum net is the population object: the infinite-dimensional limit that the sieve approximates. The population limit $n \to \infty$ sends the discrete overlapping filtration to the continuum Haag–Kastler-type net — just as $n \to \infty$ sends the sieve approximation $\sum_k \alpha_k b_k$ to the true regression function $m$.

This is the sense in which GR is the population model. The smooth manifold $M$, the continuum $\sigma$-field net, and ultimately the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ are all population-level objects — what you would see with infinite data at infinite resolution. The finite graph is the finite-sample reality.

## 3. Recovery of conformal geometry from dependence support

Once we are in the continuum limit, the dependence structure of the net determines the geometry of spacetime. The recovery proceeds in two steps — first the conformal class, then the metric scale — and both steps rest on real theorems.

The continuum $\alpha$-coefficient defines the causal structure. For regions $O_1, O_2 \subset M$:

$$O_1 \perp_s O_2 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \alpha(O_1, O_2) = 0.$$

The set $J^+(p) = \{q \in M : \alpha(U_p, U_q) > 0 \text{ for arbitrarily small neighborhoods } U_p \ni p,\, U_q \ni q\}$ is the causal future of $p$. Its boundary $\partial J^+(p)$ is the future light cone — now *defined by* the support of the dependence functional, not derived from a pre-existing metric.

Malament's theorem (1977) does the heavy lifting. In a distinguishing spacetime, the causal order determines:

1. the topology of $M$, and
2. the metric $g$ up to a conformal factor — the conformal class $[g] = \{\Omega^2 g : \Omega > 0\}$.

Earlier results by Hawking, King, and McCarthy (1976) showed that causal structure determines the topology. Applied to our framework: under the continuum limits of Axioms F1–G3, the dependence cones generated by $\alpha$ define a causal order, and this causal order determines a conformal Lorentzian structure $[g]$ on $M$.

$$\alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{causal order} \;\Longrightarrow\; [g].$$

The conformal class determines light cones, causal structure, and topology. It does not determine the overall scale — the conformal factor $\Omega^2$ that converts "angles" into "distances."

## 4. Recovery of metric scale from decay rates

The quantitative coefficient $D_{\text{BL}}$ recovers the scale.

Assume that for sufficiently small timelike-separated regions $U_p, U_q$ in the continuum limit, the dependence coefficient satisfies:

$$-\log D_{\text{BL}}(U_p, U_q) \approx \Lambda \cdot \tau(p, q) + b(p) + b(q) + o(1),$$

where $\tau(p, q)$ is the Lorentzian proper-time separation under the unknown metric $g$, $\Lambda$ is a universal constant, and $b$ is a local normalization. The logarithmic decay rate of $D_{\text{BL}}$ is controlled by proper time.

The local slope of $-\log D_{\text{BL}}$ identifies the conformal factor. Within the conformal class $[g]$ already recovered from $\alpha$, the additional information in $D_{\text{BL}}$'s decay rate pins down the representative metric:

$$D_{\text{BL}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \Omega^2 g.$$

The division of labor is complete:

$$\alpha \;\Longrightarrow\; [g] \quad \text{(qualitative: which regions are causally connected)}$$
$$D_{\text{BL}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \Omega^2 g \quad \text{(quantitative: how far apart they are)}$$

A concrete toy model makes this vivid. Consider a dependence kernel on the continuum:

$$D_{\text{BL}}(U_p, U_q) = A(p,q) \exp\!\big(-m\,\tau_g(p,q)\big) \cdot \mathbf{1}\!\big\{q \in J^+(p) \cup J^-(p)\big\},$$

where $A$ is a slowly varying amplitude and $m$ is a mass-like scale. The support of this kernel (the indicator function) gives the causal structure — hence the conformal class. The exponential rate $m \cdot \tau_g$ gives the metric scale. Everything is encoded in a single dependence kernel, read at two different levels.

In econometric terms: the *support* of the dependence kernel is the identified set; the *decay rate* is the point estimate. The first tells you which parameters are identified; the second tells you their values.

## 5. The Cauchy-temporal filtration

Once the causal order is reconstructed — either on the finite graph or in the continuum limit — the overlapping filtration can be reorganized into a temporal filtration. This is where the GR initial-value problem and the econometric time-series framework meet.

### On the finite graph

Axiom G3 provides a layering $\Sigma_0, \Sigma_1, \ldots, \Sigma_T$ of the node set. Define the discrete Cauchy-temporal filtration:

$$\mathcal{G}_t := \sigma\!\left(X_v : v \in \bigcup_{s \leq t} \Sigma_s\right).$$

This is the total information available on or before "time" $t$. It is a standard one-parameter filtration: $\mathcal{G}_s \subseteq \mathcal{G}_t$ for $s < t$. The overlapping spacetime filtration $\{\mathcal{G}(v)\}$, indexed by a partial order, is compressed into a totally ordered family $\{\mathcal{G}_t\}$ — exactly as a panel dataset with cross-sectional and temporal indices can be projected onto the time dimension alone.

### In the continuum limit

Choose a Cauchy slicing $(\Sigma_t)_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ of the limiting manifold $M$ (guaranteed by global hyperbolicity). Define:

$$\mathcal{G}_t := \sigma\!\left(\bigcup_{O \subseteq J^-(\Sigma_t)} \mathcal{F}(O)\right).$$

This is the total information available on or before the Cauchy surface $\Sigma_t$. The family $(\mathcal{G}_t)_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ is a genuine one-parameter filtration.

This is exactly the Cauchy problem of GR, restated in filtration language. In GR, global hyperbolicity guarantees that initial data on a Cauchy surface uniquely determine the solution everywhere in the domain of dependence. In our framework, $\mathcal{G}_t$ captures the same content: all information up to "time" $t$ is encoded in $\mathcal{G}_t$, and the dynamics of the filtration — how $\mathcal{G}_t$ grows — encodes the evolution of the physical system.

### The arrow of time

A filtration is inherently directional: $\mathcal{G}_t$ grows with $t$, never shrinks. This growth is what makes the future different from the past. But the source of the growth matters.

In time-series econometrics, we decompose a process into its predictable component and its innovation:

$$X_t = \mathbb{E}[X_t \mid \mathcal{G}_{t-1}] + \varepsilon_t.$$

The innovation $\varepsilon_t$ is genuinely new information at time $t$ — unpredictable from the past. The level process $X_t$ is accumulated history. The filtration grows because innovations keep arriving.

The second law of thermodynamics asserts that entropy increases with time — that the filtration's growth is accompanied by an irreversible loss of usable structure. Neither GR nor QM contains this arrow: both are time-reversible at the fundamental level. The GR field equations are symmetric under time reversal; the Schrödinger equation is unitary and invertible. Yet the macroscopic universe has a definite direction.

In filtration language, the tension is: **unitarity** says the total $\sigma$-field $\mathscr{F}$ is preserved — no information is created or destroyed. The **second law** says the observable sub-$\sigma$-field sees increasing entropy — the information becomes progressively harder to decode. Both can be simultaneously true. The innovation process adds genuinely new randomness at each step (the filtration grows), but the total information content is conserved (unitarity). What increases is the *complexity* of the encoding — the difficulty of extracting the original signal from the accumulated noise. This is the same phenomenon an econometrician encounters when a mixing process has finite memory but infinite accumulated history: the past information is "there" in principle, but practically irrecoverable from the current state.

Whether the arrow of time is a fundamental feature of the DGP (genuine innovation at each step) or an artifact of coarse-graining (deterministic dynamics that *look* random to a macroscopic observer) is the deepest question in the physics of time. The filtration framework does not answer this question, but it provides the language in which both possibilities can be stated precisely.

## 6. Toward dynamics: innovation flux and gravity

The axioms developed so far are kinematic: they describe the dependence structure and show how geometry can be recovered from it. But GR is a dynamical theory — the Einstein field equation $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G\, T_{\mu\nu}$ relates geometry to matter content. Can the dependence framework say anything about dynamics?

The direction is speculative, but it builds on a remarkable result: Jacobson's derivation (1995) of the Einstein equation from thermodynamic reasoning applied to local Rindler horizons.

### Innovation density

On the finite graph, define the local innovation at node $v$:

$$\varepsilon_v := X_v - \mathbb{E}[X_v \mid \mathcal{G}(v) \setminus \{X_v\}],$$

where $\mathcal{G}(v) \setminus \{X_v\}$ denotes the information from the causal past of $v$ excluding $v$'s own observable. The innovation variance

$$\sigma^2(v) := \text{Var}(\varepsilon_v)$$

measures the irreducible novelty at $v$ — the information content of $X_v$ that cannot be predicted from the causal past. It is the spacetime analog of the innovation variance in a time series: $\text{Var}(X_t - \mathbb{E}[X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}])$.

In a vacuum region — where "nothing happens" — the innovation variance should be small: the local observable is nearly determined by its causal past. In a region with matter or energy — where local interactions create new information — the innovation variance is large.

### Jacobson's argument, translated

Jacobson (1995) showed that the Einstein equation can be derived from three ingredients: (i) every point in spacetime admits a local Rindler horizon (an accelerating observer's boundary of accessibility), (ii) the entropy of the region is proportional to the horizon area (Bekenstein–Hawking), and (iii) the Clausius relation $\delta Q = T\,dS$ holds across the local horizon.

In the dependence framework:

- The **local horizon** is the boundary of statistical accessibility — the edge of the region from which $\mathcal{G}(v)$ draws its information.
- The **entropy** is the size of the hidden information beyond that boundary — the complexity of $\mathcal{F}(O)$ for regions $O$ outside the causal past.
- The **heat flux** is the innovation flux — the rate at which new information ($\varepsilon_v$) crosses the boundary per unit "area."
- The **curvature** is the response of the dependence cones to that flux — how the cone structure deforms when innovation density changes.

This motivates a dynamical axiom:

**Axiom D1 (Dependence-horizon balance).** Local innovation flux across infinitesimal dependence horizons deforms the cone field according to an entropy-balance law.

The target conjecture: for semiclassical information nets satisfying Axioms F1–G3 and D1, the induced Lorentzian metric $g$ obeys an Einstein-type field equation in which the effective stress-energy tensor is determined by local innovation flux.

I want to be direct about the status of this section. The kinematic framework — Sections 2 through 5 of this essay, and all of Essay 4 — rests on real mathematics: standard $\alpha$-mixing theory, the Benjamini–Schramm topology on rooted graphs, Malament's causal reconstruction theorem, the Haag–Kastler axioms in AQFT. The dynamical step — from innovation flux to the Einstein equation — is genuinely speculative. Axiom D1 is stated programmatically, not precisely. Jacobson's original argument is clean because it operates within GR's own geometric language; translating it into the dependence-theoretic framework requires substantial work that has not been done. What I can say is that the *direction* is natural: if geometry is induced from dependence, then the dynamics of geometry should be induced from the dynamics of dependence.

## 7. Complex conjugation and the limits of classical dependence

There is a subtlety in the framework that marks the boundary between what classical dependence theory can capture and what is genuinely quantum.

The dependence functionals $\alpha$ and $D_{\text{BL}}$ operate at the level of *real-valued probabilities*. They measure statistical dependence between events and between bounded real functions. This is the natural level for classical probability theory and for econometric dependence analysis.

But the underlying quantum theory operates at the level of *complex-valued amplitudes*. The passage from amplitudes to probabilities goes through conjugation: $|\langle \psi | \phi \rangle|^2 = \overline{\psi} \cdot \phi$. The Born rule — which generates the probabilities that $\alpha$ and $D_{\text{BL}}$ measure — is fundamentally a conjugate-bilinear operation.

This matters for entanglement. The canonical form of a maximally entangled bipartite state is

$$|\Phi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d}} \sum_{i=1}^{d} |i\rangle \otimes |i^*\rangle,$$

where $|i^*\rangle$ is the conjugate of $|i\rangle$ under the Riesz isomorphism $H \to H^*$. The second subsystem is literally the *conjugate* of the first. One entangled subsystem acts as the Riesz representer of the other — a connection back to Essay 1's identification of the Riesz representer as the central object of semiparametric estimation. But here the Riesz isomorphism is conjugate-linear, not linear. The phase structure — relative phases between components, interference patterns — has no direct analog in real-valued dependence theory.

There is, therefore, a third layer of dependence that the present framework does not capture: dependence at the complex amplitude level, between subsystems whose coupling goes through conjugation rather than through covariance. The $\alpha$ and $D_{\text{BL}}$ coefficients capture the Born-rule shadow of this coupling, but not the full phase structure.

This is both a limitation and a signpost. The limitation: the dependence-first axioms reconstruct geometry from the *probability structure* of quantum theory, not from the full *amplitude structure*. The signpost: any extension of the framework to full quantum gravity will need to engage the complex structure directly — to find a dependence-theoretic account of phase, interference, and the conjugate-linear pairing that creates entanglement. This is a genuinely open problem, perhaps to be explored more in one of the following essays.

## 8. What geometry owes to dependence

Let me take stock of what this essay and its predecessor have accomplished.

The two essays have proposed two shifts: a *philosophical* shift and a *mathematical* shift.

The philosophical shift is **finite first**. The primitive object of the program is a finite causal-dependence network — a DAG with local observables and partially overlapping spacetime filtrations. This is the DGP. The smooth Lorentzian manifold of general relativity is the population limit — the asymptotic effective description that emerges when the number of spacetime atoms grows large and regular. The manifold is not the starting ontology; it is the large-sample approximation.

The mathematical shift is **dependence first**. Geometry is not the stage on which physics happens. It is the summary statistic of a dependence structure — specifically, of the partially overlapping filtration and its conditional independence structure. The causal pasts of different events overlap, creating shared information (common ancestors) and private information (unique causal influences). The pattern of overlap encodes proximity; the conditional independence of local observables given the common past (Axiom L1) encodes causal disconnection; the decay of within-cone dependence (Axiom L2) encodes distance. In the continuum limit, the zero set of $\alpha$ determines the conformal class $[g]$ (via Malament's theorem), and the decay rate of $D_{\text{BL}}$ fixes the conformal factor $\Omega^2$.

The metric is a **sufficient statistic** for the local dependence kernel. It compresses the full net of $\sigma$-fields into a finite-dimensional geometric object — just as a sufficient statistic compresses a sample into a lower-dimensional summary without loss of information about the parameter. The Einstein equation is the constraint ensuring this sufficiency: the geometry must be consistent with the dependence structure that generates it.

The overlapping spacetime filtration — the family $\{\mathcal{G}(v)\}$ on the finite graph, or $\{\mathcal{G}_t\}$ in the continuum limit — is the structural thread connecting the finite DGP to the smooth population model. On the finite graph, it is a partially ordered family of $\sigma$-fields with rich overlap structure: each pair of nodes shares a common past and maintains private pasts, creating the conditional independence structure that is the probabilistic expression of causality. In the population limit, it becomes the Cauchy-temporal filtration of a globally hyperbolic spacetime, the framework within which GR's initial-value problem and time-series econometrics' filtration theory occupy the same mathematical structure. The arrow of time — the second law of thermodynamics — is the empirical fact that this filtration grows in a preferred direction, and the deepest question is whether the growth reflects genuine innovation or coarse-grained redistribution.

The two essays have identified two open frontiers. The dynamical frontier: how innovation flux across dependence horizons deforms the cone field, in the spirit of Jacobson (1995) — speculative but directionally natural. The quantum frontier: how the complex amplitude structure underlying entanglement extends beyond what classical $\alpha$-mixing can see — a genuine gap between the probability-level framework developed here and the full phase structure of quantum mechanics.

But perhaps the deepest observation is not about what remains to be done, but about what the framework already implies about the relationship between structure and information. Essay 4 observed that **what is global is the structure, not the information**: the DAG and its causal grammar organize all the local filtrations, but no single node has access to the full information of the universe. Globality belongs to law; locality belongs to information. This essay has shown how that principle extends to the continuum: the manifold and its metric are global structural objects — they organize the compatibility relations among local $\sigma$-fields — but the informational content is always local, always filtration-indexed, always accessed from a particular causal past.

This raises a complementary point: **structure without information is empty; information without structure is unintelligible.** A bare DAG with no local observables — no $X_v$, no $\sigma$-fields, no dependence — is just a combinatorial skeleton. It does not yet deserve to be called a spacetime. Conversely, a collection of random variables with no causal grammar — no overlap pattern, no conditional independence structure, no regularity conditions — is an unorganized heap of noise. What makes the framework a candidate description of reality is neither the structure alone nor the information alone, but their joint presence: a globally structured web of locally realized information, where the structure provides the language in which the information becomes meaningful, and the information provides the content that gives the structure ontological weight. In this sense, the program refines Wheeler's "it from bit": the bit only becomes "it" when embedded in a causal-dependence architecture, and the architecture only becomes real when inhabited by information-bearing events.

This series began with the thesis that **epistemology is ontology**: what exists is what can be identified and estimated. These two essays add: **spacetime geometry is an epistemological artifact**. The structure of spacetime is not a brute fact about the world; it is the structure of what can be known, locally, from the dependence among observables. And the deepest implication of the finite-first stance is that this structure is not an idealized infinity — it is a finite, stochastic, learnable DGP, whose smooth geometric limit is as much an abstraction as the population regression function is an abstraction of the finite sample.

*What exists is what can be identified from the dependence structure of a finite causal network.*

**Epistemology is ontology — in every regime, at every scale.**
