## 1. From inventory to construction

Essay 6 argued that the dependence-first framework requires a structural lifting to handle phase, interference, and entanglement. It identified the precise inventory of what is needed: an internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ at each node, a rotation operator $J$ with $J^2 = -I$, a composition rule (vector addition before norm-squaring), a conjugation operation ($J$-reflection), and the Born map from amplitudes to probabilities. It argued that these five items are equivalent to "use complex Hilbert space" but stated in a way that separates the structurally necessary from the notationally convenient.

This essay carries out the construction. It builds the lifted framework on the finite causal graph of Essay 4, defines the lifted axiom system, and shows how standard quantum phenomena — interference, entanglement, Bell violation — emerge as natural consequences of the rotational structure. It then demonstrates that the dependence framework of Essays 4–5 is recovered exactly as the Born projection of the lifted framework: the real-valued dependence structure is the shadow of a rotationally structured specification, and geometry is reconstructed from that shadow as before.

The essay works in the rotation language — the "cumbersome honesty" of the $2 \times 2$ matrix — throughout. Complex notation is used freely as shorthand where it compresses, but the structural content is always stated in terms of real 2-vectors and the operator $J$. The point is not to avoid complex numbers but to be explicit about what they are doing.

## 2. The lifted finite graph

Begin with the finite causal graph $G = (V, E)$ from Essay 4: a directed acyclic graph with vertex set $V$ (spacetime atoms) and edge set $E$ (causal links). The partial order $u \preceq v$ means $u$ is in the causal past of $v$.

**The scalar framework (Essays 4–5).** At each node $v$, attach a real-valued random variable $X_v \in \mathbb{R}$. The local $\sigma$-field is $\mathcal{F}(v) = \sigma(X_v)$, the past $\sigma$-field is $\mathcal{G}(v) = \sigma(X_u : u \preceq v)$, and the axioms (L1, L2, etc.) govern the conditional independence and dependence decay among these $\sigma$-fields.

**The lifted framework.** At each node $v$, replace the scalar $X_v$ with a **2-vector**:

$$
Y_v = \begin{pmatrix} X_v^{(1)} \\ X_v^{(2)} \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^2,
$$

and equip each internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the rotation operator

$$
J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad J^2 = -I.
$$

The pair $(Y_v, J)$ is the lifted local state. In complex shorthand, this is the complex amplitude $\psi_v = X_v^{(1)} + i X_v^{(2)}$, but the real 2-vector formulation is the primitive.

The internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ at each node is the **amplitude fiber** — the local specification-layer state space. It carries the angular degree of freedom (phase) that the scalar framework lacked. The rotation $e^{\theta J}$ acts on this fiber, generating the $SO(2) \cong U(1)$ phase symmetry.

## 3. Two layers of $\sigma$-fields

The lifting creates two distinct families of $\sigma$-fields at each node.

**Specification-layer $\sigma$-fields.** The full information generated by the 2-vector:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v) = \sigma(X_v^{(1)}, X_v^{(2)}) = \sigma(Y_v).
$$

This $\sigma$-field sees everything about the local amplitude — both the modulus and the phase. It lives at the specification layer.

**Information-layer $\sigma$-fields (Born-projected).** The information generated by the Born-projected observable:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) = \sigma(\|Y_v\|^2) = \sigma\bigl((X_v^{(1)})^2 + (X_v^{(2)})^2\bigr).
$$

This $\sigma$-field sees only the squared modulus — the probability-level content. It is strictly coarser than $\mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v)$:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) \subset \mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v).
$$

The inclusion is strict precisely because the Born map discards phase. Two amplitude states with the same modulus but different angles generate the same $\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}$ but different $\mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}$. This is the formal expression of the phase non-identifiability discussed in Essay 6, Section 9.

More generally, for any local Hermitian observable $A_v$ (a real symmetric $2 \times 2$ matrix acting on the amplitude fiber), the Born-projected measurement is

$$
\langle A_v \rangle = Y_v^T A_v Y_v,
$$

which is a real-valued quadratic form in the 2-vector $Y_v$. The information-layer $\sigma$-field can be enriched by including multiple such observables:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) = \sigma\bigl(Y_v^T A Y_v : A \in \mathscr{A}_v\bigr),
$$

where $\mathscr{A}_v$ is the collection of local Hermitian operators available at node $v$. With a sufficiently rich set of observables, the information-layer $\sigma$-field can recover more of the specification-layer content — this is the formal version of "phase becomes identifiable through multiple projections."

**Past $\sigma$-fields.** As in Essay 4, define the accumulated past:

$$
\mathcal{G}^{\text{spec}}(v) = \sigma(Y_u : u \preceq v), \qquad
\mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}(v) = \sigma\bigl(Y_u^T A Y_u : u \preceq v,\, A \in \mathscr{A}_u\bigr).
$$

The specification-layer past accumulates full amplitude information along the causal past. The information-layer past accumulates Born-projected measurement data. The dependence framework of Essays 4–5 operates on the information-layer family $\{\mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}(v)\}$. The lifted framework adds the specification-layer family $\{\mathcal{G}^{\text{spec}}(v)\}$ above it.

The characteristic function analogy from Essay 6 makes this two-layer structure concrete. The characteristic function $\varphi(t)$ of a distribution is complex-valued and globally defined — it encodes the full distributional content, including moments of all orders. The density $f(x)$ is real-valued and locally estimable. Nobody confuses the two, and nobody expects conditional independence to hold at the level of characteristic functions rather than densities. The specification-layer $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v)$ are the analogue of working with the full characteristic function; the information-layer $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v)$ are the analogue of working with the density. The Born map $\|Y_v\|^2$ is the analogue of the inverse Fourier transform that takes $\varphi(t)$ to $f(x)$: it projects from the complex specification to the real estimable object.

## 4. The lifted axiom system

Essay 4 introduced axioms F1–F2 (filtration structure), L1–L2 (local dependence), and G1–G3 (global regularity). The lifted framework preserves and refines them.

### Axiom F1$'$ (Lifted filtration structure)

At each node $v \in V$, the local state is a 2-vector $Y_v \in \mathbb{R}^2$ equipped with the rotation operator $J$. The specification-layer $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v)$ and $\mathcal{G}^{\text{spec}}(v)$ are defined as above.

### Axiom F2$'$ (Born projection)

At each node $v$, a collection of local Hermitian operators $\mathscr{A}_v$ defines the Born-projected information-layer $\sigma$-fields $\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v)$ and $\mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}(v)$.

### Axiom L1$'$ (Conditional independence at the information layer)

This is the key axiom. Conditional independence — spacelike factorization — belongs to the **information layer**, not the specification layer. For spacelike-separated nodes $u, v$ with common past $C(u,v) = \{w : w \preceq u \text{ and } w \preceq v\}$:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(u) \perp \mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) \;\big|\; \mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}\bigl(C(u,v)\bigr).
$$

This is the same conditional independence as Essay 4's Axiom L1, but now stated explicitly as an information-layer property. The Born-projected observables at spacelike-separated nodes are conditionally independent given the common Born-projected past.

Crucially, **the specification-layer $\sigma$-fields need not satisfy this factorization.** The global amplitude state $\psi$ may be entangled across spacelike-separated nodes at the specification level — this is exactly what entanglement is. But the entanglement is invisible to the information layer except through the correlations it induces in the Born-projected observables. And those correlations do satisfy conditional independence given the common past, as Essay 4 established.

This is the formal realization of Essay 6's central thesis: **conditional independence belongs to data, not to basis functions.** The specification layer carries entanglement. The information layer carries conditional independence. They coexist because they are different layers.

### Axiom L2$'$ (Cone-supported weak dependence at the information layer)

For causally connected nodes, the bounded-Lipschitz dependence coefficient $D_{\text{BL}}$ is defined on the information-layer $\sigma$-fields and decays with graph distance:

$$
D_{\text{BL}}\bigl(\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(u),\, \mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) \mid \mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}(C)\bigr) \leq C_0 \, e^{-\lambda \, d(u,v)}.
$$

This is again an information-layer property. The geometry-reconstruction program of Essay 5 uses $D_{\text{BL}}$ decay to recover metric scale. Since $D_{\text{BL}}$ is computed from Born-projected observables, the geometric reconstruction proceeds exactly as before. The lifting does not disturb it.

### Axiom J (Rotational structure)

This is the genuinely new axiom. Each amplitude fiber $\mathbb{R}^2_v$ is equipped with the operator $J_v$ satisfying $J_v^2 = -I_v$. This is the structural content of the complex lift. It is the only new axiom — everything else is inherited from the scalar framework, applied to the information layer.

### Axioms G1$'$–G3$'$ (Global regularity)

The global regularity axioms (uniform local structure, bounded degree, stationarity under Benjamini–Schramm limits) carry over with obvious modifications: the local state space is now $\mathbb{R}^2$ rather than $\mathbb{R}$, and the uniformity conditions apply to the lifted structure including $J$.

## 5. Amplitude propagation on the DAG

In the scalar framework, the "dynamics" were implicit: the DAG structure and the axioms determined the dependence structure. In the lifted framework, amplitude propagation along causal edges can be made explicit.

For each directed edge $(u, v) \in E$ — meaning $u$ is a direct causal predecessor of $v$ — define a **propagator**: a $2 \times 2$ real matrix $T_{u \to v}$ that maps the amplitude at $u$ to its contribution at $v$:

$$
T_{u \to v} \in \text{Mat}_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{R}).
$$

If $T_{u \to v}$ commutes with $J$ — i.e., if it has the form $aI + bJ$ for some $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$ — then it corresponds to multiplication by the complex number $a + ib$. This is the **$J$-compatible** case: the propagator respects the rotational structure.

If $T_{u \to v}$ is additionally orthogonal (norm-preserving), it is a rotation $e^{\theta J}$, and the propagation is unitary — it preserves the amplitude norm. This is the special case discussed in Essay 6, Section 8: unitarity at the specification layer.

The amplitude at a node $v$ with multiple predecessors $u_1, \ldots, u_k$ is determined by the composition rule:

$$
Y_v = \sum_{j=1}^{k} T_{u_j \to v} \, Y_{u_j} + \varepsilon_v,
$$

where $\varepsilon_v \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is the **local innovation** — the new stochastic information entering at node $v$. This is the lifted analogue of the autoregressive representation on a DAG. In the scalar framework, one would write $X_v = \sum_j a_j X_{u_j} + \varepsilon_v$ with scalar coefficients. The lifted version replaces scalars with $2 \times 2$ matrices and innovations with 2-vectors.

The local innovation $\varepsilon_v$ is the source of the arrow of time at the information layer: it is the new randomness that enters the filtration at node $v$ and cannot be un-observed. At the specification layer, the propagators $T_{u \to v}$ may be norm-preserving (unitary), but the innovations $\varepsilon_v$ introduce genuine stochastic irreversibility. This is the formal realization of Essay 6, Section 8: **reversibility is a property of the specification (the propagators); irreversibility is a property of the information (the innovations).**

## 6. Interference on the graph

Interference arises when two causal paths from a source node $s$ to a target node $t$ contribute amplitudes that add before norm-squaring.

Suppose $s$ has two causal successors $u_1$ and $u_2$, both of which are predecessors of $t$. The amplitude contributed to $t$ via each path is:

$$
\vec{\alpha}_1 = T_{u_1 \to t} \, T_{s \to u_1} \, Y_s, \qquad
\vec{\alpha}_2 = T_{u_2 \to t} \, T_{s \to u_2} \, Y_s.
$$

The total amplitude at $t$ (from $s$ via both paths) is:

$$
Y_t^{(s)} = \vec{\alpha}_1 + \vec{\alpha}_2,
$$

and the Born-projected probability is:

$$
P = \|Y_t^{(s)}\|^2 = \|\vec{\alpha}_1\|^2 + \|\vec{\alpha}_2\|^2 + 2(\vec{\alpha}_1 \cdot \vec{\alpha}_2).
$$

The cross-term $2(\vec{\alpha}_1 \cdot \vec{\alpha}_2)$ is the interference term. It depends on the relative orientation of the two amplitude 2-vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$, which is determined by the product of the propagator matrices along each path.

Concretely: if $T_1 = T_{u_1 \to t} T_{s \to u_1}$ and $T_2 = T_{u_2 \to t} T_{s \to u_2}$ are the composed propagators along each path, then

$$
\vec{\alpha}_1 \cdot \vec{\alpha}_2 = Y_s^T T_1^T T_2 \, Y_s.
$$

The matrix $T_1^T T_2$ encodes the relative rotation between the two paths. If both paths impose the same total rotation (i.e., $T_1 = T_2$), the cross-term is maximally positive — perfect constructive interference. If one path rotates by an additional $\pi$ relative to the other (i.e., $T_1 = -T_2$), the cross-term is maximally negative — perfect destructive interference. Intermediate cases produce partial interference.

This is the **double-slit structure on a graph**. The DAG provides the causal paths; the propagator matrices provide the phase accumulation along each path; the vector-addition-before-norm-squaring rule produces the interference pattern. No complex numbers appear — only $2 \times 2$ real matrices, 2-vectors, and inner products. But the result is exactly the quantum interference formula.

The classical limit is the case where all propagators are scalar multiples of the identity: $T_{u \to v} = a_{uv} I$ with $a_{uv} \in \mathbb{R}$. Then the amplitudes are collinear (both point along the same direction in $\mathbb{R}^2$), the cross-term is always non-negative, and destructive interference is impossible. **Interference requires that the propagators have a non-trivial $J$-component** — that is, that they perform genuine rotations in the internal plane, not just scalings.

The characteristic function analogy from Essay 6 maps cleanly onto this structure. Composing propagators along a path — $T_2 T_1$ — is like multiplying characteristic functions along a chain of independent contributions: the natural specification-layer operation is multiplicative and rotational. The Born map that projects the resulting 2-vector amplitude to a probability is the analogue of the inverse Fourier transform that recovers a density from a characteristic function. Interference is what happens when two specification-layer paths are composed and then projected, rather than projected and then composed.

## 7. Entanglement on the graph

Entanglement in the lifted framework is the $J$-reflection pairing between the amplitude fibers at two nodes.

Consider two nodes $u$ and $v$ that share a common ancestor $s$ but are spacelike-separated (neither is in the other's causal past). In the scalar framework, Axiom L1 says their local observables are conditionally independent given the common past. In the lifted framework, this remains true at the information layer. But at the specification layer, the amplitude 2-vectors $Y_u$ and $Y_v$ may be **correlated through the common ancestor**, with correlations that carry rotational structure.

Specifically, the joint amplitude state of $(Y_u, Y_v)$, conditioned on $Y_s$ at the common ancestor, is determined by the propagators along the two paths. If $T_1 = T_{s \to u}$ and $T_2 = T_{s \to v}$ are the propagators, then

$$
Y_u = T_1 Y_s + \varepsilon_u, \qquad Y_v = T_2 Y_s + \varepsilon_v,
$$

and the specification-layer covariance (conditioned on everything except the innovations) is:

$$
\text{Cov}^{\text{spec}}(Y_u, Y_v) = T_1 \, \text{Var}(Y_s) \, T_2^T.
$$

This is a $2 \times 2$ matrix — not a scalar. It carries rotational information through the propagators $T_1$ and $T_2$.

**When is this entanglement?** In the scalar framework, the analogous quantity $\text{Cov}(X_u, X_v) = a_1 \text{Var}(X_s) a_2$ is a scalar, and it can be fully explained by a shared hidden variable ($X_s$). But in the lifted framework, the $2 \times 2$ covariance matrix $T_1 \text{Var}(Y_s) T_2^T$ can have off-diagonal structure that involves $J$ — specifically, it can have a non-zero **antisymmetric part**:

$$
\text{Cov}^{\text{spec}}(Y_u, Y_v) = \underbrace{S}_{\text{symmetric}} + \underbrace{A}_{\text{antisymmetric}},
$$

where $S = S^T$ and $A = -A^T$. The symmetric part $S$ corresponds to the real part of the complex covariance — the "classical" correlation. The antisymmetric part $A$ corresponds to the imaginary part — the **symplectic correlation** $\omega(Y_u, Y_v)$.

The antisymmetric part is the structural signature of entanglement. It arises when the propagators $T_1$ and $T_2$ rotate the amplitude by different angles — that is, when they have different $J$-components. A classical (scalar) hidden-variable model, by construction, produces only symmetric covariance. The antisymmetric part is what a scalar model cannot produce. It is the content of the $J$-reflection pairing: the first subsystem's amplitude vector and the second subsystem's $J$-reflected amplitude vector are correlated through the symplectic form $\omega$.

**Maximal entanglement** occurs when the two propagators are related by $J$-reflection: $T_2 = T_1 J^T$, so that the second path rotates the amplitude by exactly $\pi/2$ relative to the first. In this case, the covariance matrix is purely antisymmetric, and the specification-layer state is the canonical entangled pair from Essay 6: one subsystem paired with the rotation-reversed copy of the other.

## 8. The Bell inequality in the lifted framework

The Bell inequality provides the empirical test that separates the scalar framework from the lifted one.

Consider the standard Bell–CHSH setup: two spacelike-separated nodes $u$ and $v$, each with a choice of two measurement settings. At node $u$, the observer can measure one of two Hermitian operators $A_1$ or $A_2$ on the amplitude fiber $\mathbb{R}^2_u$. At node $v$, the observer can measure $B_1$ or $B_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^2_v$. Each measurement produces a Born-projected outcome $\pm 1$.

Define the CHSH combination:

$$
\mathcal{S} = \langle A_1 B_1 \rangle + \langle A_1 B_2 \rangle + \langle A_2 B_1 \rangle - \langle A_2 B_2 \rangle,
$$

where $\langle A_i B_j \rangle = \mathbb{E}[(Y_u^T A_i Y_u)(Y_v^T B_j Y_v)]$ is the correlation of the Born-projected measurements.

**In the scalar framework** (one-dimensional internal states, no $J$): the Bell–CHSH inequality gives $|\mathcal{S}| \leq 2$. This bound applies to any model where the correlations arise from shared scalar hidden variables.

**In the lifted framework** (two-dimensional internal states with $J$): the Tsirelson bound gives $|\mathcal{S}| \leq 2\sqrt{2}$. The violation of 2 — and saturation of $2\sqrt{2}$ — is achievable precisely when the state has non-zero antisymmetric (symplectic) covariance and the measurement operators have non-trivial $J$-components.

In the rotation language, the mechanism is transparent. The Bell bound of 2 is the maximum achievable when the internal state space is one-dimensional: all amplitude vectors are collinear, all correlations are symmetric, and the measurement operators are scalar multiples of the identity. The quantum violation arises from the **second dimension** — the $J$-component of the amplitude vectors and the $J$-component of the measurement operators. The extra correlations come from the antisymmetric (symplectic) part of the covariance, which is accessible only when the internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ is actively used.

So the Bell violation is not a mystery about nonlocality or retrocausality. It is a **dimensional phenomenon**: the internal state space is two-dimensional with a rotational structure, and this extra dimension allows correlations that a one-dimensional model cannot produce. The information-layer conditional independence (Axiom L1$'$) is preserved — no signal travels faster than light. The specification-layer rotational correlations, propagated through the common ancestor, produce the violation.

Which Bell assumption does the framework relax? Not locality — information-layer conditional independence holds exactly. Not the freedom of measurement choice. What is relaxed is the assumption that the joint state admits a **classical scalar probability model** over pre-existing outcomes. In the Bell–Fine framework, the hidden variable $\lambda$ assigns a definite scalar outcome to each measurement setting, and the joint distribution $P(\lambda)$ generates all correlations. In the lifted framework, the specification-layer state is a 2-vector with rotational structure, and its covariance has an antisymmetric (symplectic) part that no scalar joint distribution can produce. The "hidden state" is not a classical random variable but an amplitude vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ — richer than any scalar model, yet not nonlocal.

This is where the global sieve versus local information separation clarifies what would otherwise seem paradoxical. The specification layer — the amplitude field $\{Y_v, J\}$ — is global: it is the DGP's basis-function expansion, analogous to Fourier modes that extend over all of space and time. Entanglement is a structural feature of this global specification, established when the state was prepared at the common ancestor. The information layer — the Born-projected filtration — is local: it respects conditional independence, causal support, and the light-cone boundary. The Bell violation lives at the specification layer; the no-signaling theorem lives at the information layer. There is no conflict because they describe different layers. A physicist who asks "how can correlations violate Bell without nonlocality?" is asking a specification-layer question and expecting an information-layer answer. The layers must be kept separate.

## 9. Recovery of the dependence framework

A central requirement of the lifting is that it must be **consistent** with the dependence framework of Essays 4–5. The geometry-reconstruction program — $\alpha_C$ determines $[g]$, $D_{\text{BL}}$ determines $\Omega^2 g$ — must survive intact.

This is straightforward. The dependence framework operates on the information-layer $\sigma$-fields $\{\mathcal{G}^{\text{info}}(v)\}$. The lifted framework adds the specification-layer $\sigma$-fields $\{\mathcal{G}^{\text{spec}}(v)\}$ above them but does not modify the information-layer structure. Since:

$$
\mathcal{F}^{\text{info}}(v) \subset \mathcal{F}^{\text{spec}}(v),
$$

the information-layer $\sigma$-fields are a coarsening of the specification-layer $\sigma$-fields. All the axioms of Essays 4–5 (L1, L2, G1–G3) hold at the information layer, as stated in Axioms L1$'$ and L2$'$ above. The dependence coefficients $\alpha_C$ and $D_{\text{BL}}$ are computed from Born-projected observables, and the geometric reconstruction proceeds exactly as before.

What the lifting adds is the **explanation** of why the information-layer dependence has the structure it does. In the scalar framework, the dependence structure was a primitive — it was simply postulated via axioms. In the lifted framework, the information-layer dependence is **derived** from the specification-layer amplitude propagation:

$$
\text{specification-layer amplitudes} + \text{propagators} + J
\;\xrightarrow{\text{Born map}}\;
\text{information-layer dependence structure}.
$$

The $\alpha$-mixing coefficients, for instance, are determined by the propagator matrices and the local innovations. Two nodes are information-layer independent (given the common past) precisely when the specification-layer correlations, after Born projection, produce a factorizing probability law. This is guaranteed by Axiom L1$'$ for spacelike-separated nodes.

The lifting therefore achieves a satisfying division of labor:

- **Essays 4–5 (scalar framework)**: describe the information-layer structure and reconstruct geometry from dependence. This is the epistemological content.
- **Essay 7 (lifted framework)**: describes the specification-layer structure that generates the information-layer dependence. This is the DGP itself, richer than its Born shadow, carrying phase and entanglement.
- **The Born map**: connects the two, projecting the richer specification onto the observable information layer.

The structure is the same as the relationship between a characteristic function, a density, and data: the characteristic function $\varphi(t)$ (complex, global) determines the density $f(x)$ (real, locally estimable), which determines the observable dependence structure from which geometry is reconstructed. The amplitude field $\{Y_v, J\}$ is the characteristic function; the Born-projected dependence structure is the density; the realized filtration is the data.

## 10. What the lifting does and does not claim

Let me be explicit about the scope of this construction.

**What it does:**

1. It provides a concrete, finite, mechanistic model in which quantum phenomena (phase, interference, entanglement, Bell violation) arise from a minimal structural lifting: an internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ with rotation operator $J$ at each node of the causal DAG.

2. It preserves the entire dependence-first program of Essays 4–5 at the information layer. Geometry reconstruction is undisturbed.

3. It gives a formal realization of the layered ontology of Essay 6: global structural grammar (the DAG), global complex sieve (the amplitude field $\{Y_v\}$ with propagators), Born-projected probabilities, and local information (the filtration).

4. It identifies the precise structural content of "complex quantum mechanics": two-dimensional internal state space + rotation + vector-addition composition rule + $J$-reflection for conjugation. Nothing more is needed.

**What it does not claim:**

1. It does not derive quantum mechanics from first principles. The lifted axioms (F1$'$, F2$'$, L1$'$, L2$'$, J) are postulated, not derived. The construction shows that the lifting is *consistent and minimal*, not that it is *unique or necessary*.

2. It does not solve the measurement problem in the foundational sense. The Born map is postulated (Axiom F2$'$), not derived from a deeper principle. Why the specification layer projects onto the information layer through norm-squaring rather than some other map remains an open question.

3. It does not address the continuum limit of the lifted framework. Essay 5 took the scalar framework to its Benjamini–Schramm limit and recovered continuum geometry. The analogous limit for the lifted framework — recovering continuum quantum field theory from a lifted finite graph — is a much harder problem, left for future work.

4. It does not claim that the $\mathbb{R}^2$ fiber with $J$ is the final word. Higher-dimensional fibers (corresponding to higher-spin representations, $SU(2)$, $SU(3)$, etc.) are natural generalizations. The $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $SO(2)$ is the simplest case — the $U(1)$ phase structure of basic quantum mechanics. The framework is designed to be extensible.

## 11. The reconciliation

This essay completes the arc that began with Essay 4.

Essay 4 built the finite causal-dependence network: a DAG with scalar observables, overlapping $\sigma$-fields, and axioms governing conditional independence and dependence decay. Essay 5 took this to the population limit and recovered spacetime geometry. Essay 6 identified the elephant in the room — the complex amplitude structure that the scalar framework cannot see — and proposed a clean separation between global sieve specification and local information, connected by the Born map. This essay has carried out the formal construction: the lifted finite graph with internal $\mathbb{R}^2$ and rotation operator $J$.

The reconciliation with existing quantum mechanics is exact in the following sense: the lifted framework, with $J$-compatible propagators and Born-projected information-layer $\sigma$-fields, reproduces all the standard quantum phenomena on a finite causal graph. Phase is the angular degree of freedom in the internal $\mathbb{R}^2$. Interference is vector addition before norm-squaring. Entanglement is $J$-reflection pairing through common ancestors. The Bell violation is a dimensional phenomenon — the second dimension of the internal state space. Unitarity is norm-preservation of the propagators — a natural but not mandatory property of the specification layer.

And the entire scalar dependence framework sits inside the lifted one as the Born projection. The information layer is the shadow of the specification layer. Geometry is reconstructed from the shadow. What the lifting adds is the specification of what casts the shadow: a rotationally structured amplitude field on the finite causal graph.

**Epistemology is ontology — and now the ontology has an internal rotation.**
